I went to WebExpo Prague with the University of Limerick to skill-maxx for the web apps and SaaS ideas I'm building. I got that, but I also ended up network-maxxing far more than expected.

## More than a web conference

WebExpo sat in a useful middle ground between technology, product, design, and business. Talks covered AI, UX, frontend work, design systems, accessibility, security, marketing, and sales. The common question was what building a good digital product looks like now.

That mix was exactly what I wanted. As a Computer Science student, it is easy to develop tunnel vision around code. I enjoy getting buried in a bug until it finally gives up, but a real product also depends on users, trust, positioning, design, pricing, and distribution. Shipping a feature is not progress if it solves no real problem.

The talks were useful, but conversations made the ideas stick. Speaker corners, the Mentor Café, sponsor booths, and parties turned the event into more than a sequence of presentations. I spoke with people from America, Czechia, Germany, Azerbaijan, Ireland, the Netherlands, and probably a few places I have forgotten.

## The AI thread

The AI discussions matched [what I had already been thinking about development](/blog/ai-opinion). I use these tools heavily, but as a speed multiplier rather than a replacement for understanding. The useful skill is still being able to steer, question, test, and take responsibility for the result.

WebExpo added an important product angle: building faster only helps when you understand what should be built and why.

## Prague and the people

Outside the conference, Prague was class. The architecture was gorgeous, the food was lovely, and the beer was cheap enough to make Ireland look like a badly priced side quest—even though I remain a whiskey person at heart. Public transport was so functional it felt like a personal attack: trams arrived, metro lines made sense, and people got places. Horrifying behaviour to anyone used to Irish transport.

I arrived barely knowing anyone on the University of Limerick trip. I had briefly spoken to one person on Discord; that was about it. By the end, I had made a load of friends and learnt to say yes instead of hovering at the edge of the room.

At [FOSDEM in Brussels](/blog/fosdem), I learnt that a conference can be a useful reason to explore somewhere new. Prague reinforced the point without needing another grand travel manifesto: the badge gets me there, but the people and city make the trip memorable.
