I went to WebExpo Prague with University of Limerick to skill maxx for the web apps and SaaS ideas I'm building.

I did get that. But I also ended up network maxxing far more than expected.

WebExpo sat in a nice middle ground between tech conference, product conference, design conference, and business event. There was a lot of AI and UX/UI, but also frontend, product thinking, design systems, accessibility, security, marketing, sales, sponsors, and people trying to figure out what building digital products actually looks like now.

That mix was exactly what I wanted. As a Computer Science student it is very easy to get tunnel vision on the code. I like code. I like systems. I like getting buried in a bug until it finally gives up. But if I want to build real products, especially web apps and SaaS, code is only one piece of it.

You also need to care about users. Positioning. Trust. Design. Pricing. Distribution. Whether the thing you are building solves a real problem, or whether you are just shipping a feature because it felt productive at the time.

That kept coming up for me at WebExpo. The talks were useful, but the conversations around them made the ideas stick. Chats in the speaker corners, the Mentor Cafe, sponsor booths, and the parties turned the event into something much more useful than just sitting in rooms taking notes.

Some conversations were about AI, startups, design, engineering, careers, and how people actually work. Others were about travel, food, university life, culture, and how people ended up where they are.

I met people from America, Czechia, Germany, Azerbaijan, Ireland, the Netherlands, and probably a few places I am forgetting. That is the part of travel I always end up loving most. You can be in a city for a few days and suddenly your idea of what "normal" looks like gets stretched by every conversation.

The AI side was especially interesting because it matched a lot of what I have been thinking about already.

I use AI a lot. I am not trying to pretend otherwise. But I do not see it as magic and I definitely do not see it as a replacement for understanding. It is a speed boost. A multiplier. If you know what you are doing, it helps you move much faster. If you do not, it can just multiply confusion.

That is the line I keep coming back to with AI in development. The useful people are not the ones who blindly paste a prompt and ship whatever comes back. The useful people are the ones who can steer, question, correct, test, and understand the system while the tool does some of the heavy lifting.

AI makes building faster. It does not remove the need to know what you are building or why.

Outside the conference, Prague was class.

The architecture is gorgeous. The food was lovely. The beer was cheap enough to make Ireland look like a badly priced side quest, even though I remain a whiskey person at heart. The public transport was so functional it felt like a personal attack. Trams just arrived. Metro lines made sense. People got places. Horrifying behaviour from the perspective of someone used to Irish public transport.

I also came over barely knowing anyone on the UL trip. I had briefly spoken to one person beforehand on Discord, and that was about it. By the end, I had made a load of new friends and got a very fast lesson in speed bonding, inter-society craic, and saying yes to things instead of hovering around the edge of the room.

That mattered more than I expected.

I have always liked the idea of travel, but trips like this remind me that I do not just want to visit places for the sake of ticking them off a list. I want to meet people from different parts of the world. I want to see new architecture and landscapes. I want to try different food, hear different accents, learn how people live, and collect the kind of stories that only happen when you leave your usual bubble.

That is a big part of why Computer Science, development, and IT appeal to me.

I like building things. I like systems. I like the internet. But I also like that this field can be portable. It can put you in rooms with people from everywhere. It can give you a reason to go to Brussels for open source, Prague for WebExpo, or wherever the next useful excuse appears.

FOSDEM was the excuse for Brussels. WebExpo was the excuse for Prague.

The real value was the people, the city, the conversations, and the reminder that I want a life where those things keep happening.
